About me



I am an bioinformatician broadly interested in environmental microbiology and metagenomics.

My research experience encompass different topics and interests. I realized a master's degree in fundamental and applied microbiology at the university of Rennes 1, France. During this master's degree, I realized an intership placement at the university of Tennesse, USA in the laboratory of Dr. S.W. Wilhelm, working on phage-host interactions between cyanobacteria and cyanophages.

I went back to France to realize a PhD in molecular and cellular biology in the CEA (center for atomic energy) of Saclay, France. My dissertation focused on understanding intracellular fluxes of glutathion and its impact on protein secretion in yeast.

Convinced of the importance of computer science and data analysis skills in today's biological research, I decided to complete a second master's degree in computer science, at the university of Rennes 1, France. During this time, I add the opportunity to perform a placement at the University of Arizona, in the laboratory of Dr. Bonnie Hurwitz. At the end of my master's degree, I joined her lab as a postdoctoral fellow, working on several projects and tools for the analysis of metagenomics datasets.

Current research interests


Viral hunting in metagenomes


Understanding the role and distribution of viruses in ecosystems is critical and metagenomics offers new avenues. To explore the viral biodiversity and ecology, a number of bioinformatic tools perform a high-level taxonomic (viral or cellular origin) assignment of metagenomic sequences. These tools aim to allow to collect all viral sequences in a metagenome and help the discovery of new viral groups. While many tools rely on references databases, new approaches using machine learning and pattern detection techniques have been suggested. Indeed, machine learning model can be trained to identify a set of features that signal a viral origin to generalize the identification of all viral sequences.
As users of these tools, we aim to explore the promise and current limitations of viral detection in metagenomic datasets.

Read Ponsero and Hurwitz (2019) The promises and pitfalls of machine learning for detecting viruses in aquatic metagenomes, Frontiers in Microbiology
Read Hurwitz , Ponsero, Thornton, U’Ren (2017) Phage Hunters: computational strategies for finding phages in large-scale ‘omics datasets, Virus research (Review article)

kmer-based de novo metagenomic comparisons


Over the last decade, scientists have generated petabytes of metagenomic data allowing an unprecedented description of the role of microbes in their ecosystems. However, to understand the underlying biological principles that guide the distribution of microbial communities, massive ‘omics datasets need to be compared with environmental factors to find linkages across space and time. In order to examine the complete genomic content, metagenomic samples can be compared using their sequence signature (or frequency of k-mers). These de novo approaches allow the comparison of complete metagenomes set to each other without relying on annotation or assembly.

Read Choi, Ponsero, Bomhoff, Youens-Clark, Hartman and Hurwitz (2019) Libra: scalable k-mer based tool for massive all-vs-all metagenome comparisons, GigaScience
Read Choi, Ponsero, Bomhoff, Youens-Clark, Hartman and Hurwitz (2018) Libra: Improved Partitioning Strategies for Massive Comparative Metagenomics Analysis. In Proceedings of the 9th Workshop on Scientific Cloud Computing

FAIR principle applied to metagenomic datasets


Oceanographic research cruises are collaborative efforts providing critical physical, chemical, and biological data ('omics data and non-omics measurements). These research expeditions span across a broad array of disciplines and are critical to investigating the interplay between biological, geological, and chemical processes in the ocean.
Importantly, although scientists carefully curate and share their data with collaborators to advance individual studies and publications, no systematic, unifying framework currently exists to integrate ‘omics data with physical, geological, geochemical, and biological datasets standardly used by the broader geoscience community. In this context, the development of resources to promote the publication of oceanic ‘omics datasets along with their physio-chemical information is critical.
We aim to develop Planet Microbe, a web platform for the integration of 'omics datasets in their broader environmental context. Moreover, the project aims to provide community tools to support the publication and analysis of new oceanic datasets. Modern data publication requires the user to meet FAIR data standards to ensure data are Findable Accessible, Interoperable, and Reusable (FAIR), however, few tools are currently available for the user to ensure the data are meeting these important standards. Planet Microbe aims to provide tools to evaluate private datasets against these standards and promote the integration of new data in standard data repositories.

Past research interests


redox biology and protein secretion in the yeast S. cerevisiae


The endoplasmic reticulum is the first intracellular compartment of the protein secretion pathway. Protein maturation in this compartment involves protein folding and post-traductionnal modification including formation of disulfide bonds. The formation of disulfide bonds is operated by a highly conserved redox relay made of the thiol oxidase Ero1 and the protein disulfide isomerase (PDI). Ero1 catalyzes disulfide bond formation and relays them by thiol-disulfide exchange to PDI, which in turn oxidizes substrates. Isomerization and terminal reduction of non-native disulfide bonds both rely on a reduction system that remains to be formally identified. Studies however suggest the importance of reduced glutathione in this reducing system. GSH is small redox tripeptide exclusively synthesized in the cytosol. My PhD project aimed to (i) describe the main parameters of glutathione traffic across the ER membrane (ii) identify the main actors involved in the transport and (iii) analyze the physiological impact of a modification of the ER glutathione redox state


Read Ponsero, Igbaria, Darch, Miled, Outten, Winther, Palais, D’Autréaux, Delaunay-Moisan andToledano (2017) Endoplasmic Reticulum Transport of Glutathione by Sec61 Is Regulated by Ero1 and Bip. Molecular Cell
Read Delaunay-Moisan , Ponsero, Toledano (2017) Reexamining the function of glutathione in oxidative protein folding and secretion. Antioxidant Redox Signaling (Review article)
Read my PhD thesis (2016) Contrôle redox de la sécrétion protéique chez Saccharomyces cerevisiae (In French)

Scientific heritage


Short project in collaboration with the CNB (cercle Naturaliste Brestois) to evaluate and index the academic herbarium from the University of Brest (UBO). The herbarium, compting more than 30.000 specimens was collected by Claude and Mireille Moreau at the time of the University's creation around 1960. The project allowed to shed light on the critical condition of some collections, left unsuppervised for years. The conclusion of this inventory work was published in a botanical journal (see below) and presented to a conference on scientific heritage (colloque Patrimoine scientifique en Bretagne, 2009).
